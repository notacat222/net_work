‘Brain’ meets the viewer with a mirror reflection of the human brain, a comment on the intimate relationship one holds with their devices. How a computer over time, becomes a mirror of their owner’s brain, and the power computers have in mimicking human brain pathways.

Machines are able to do this through their operating network systems, an interconnected web of parts. The work presents a data set of a brain, visualizing and interacting with different senses and parts. The parametric equation serves as a metaphor for one system, that is built from and with other parts, a network that would not be able to operate by itself.

The interaction awakens a post-humanist perspective, prompting the viewer to visualise how they might fit into a network, experience and consciousness. The work is an interrogation of what lies beneath, the effect of one manipulation and an examination of shared humanity, creating a contact zone between the human and computer where the agency of each is questioned.

‘Brain’ asks the viewer to think of their social impact through contextualising their interconnectedness, and questions the damage of viewing data and elements in singularity, awakening a shared, invisible collective consciousness.
